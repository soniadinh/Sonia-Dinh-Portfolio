Laws of UX

Laws of UX is a collection of best practices that designers can consider when building user interfaces. These principles, rooted in psychology and human behavior, help create intuitive and effective user experiences. Understanding these laws enables designers to anticipate how users will interact with their products and make informed design decisions.

These laws are not strict rules but rather guidelines that have been observed and validated through years of research and practice. They provide a framework for creating designs that feel natural and reduce cognitive load for users.

Key Principles

  • User-Centered Design: Always prioritize the needs, behaviors, and limitations of the end user.
  • Cognitive Load: Minimize the mental effort required to use your interface by simplifying choices and reducing clutter.
  • Consistency: Maintain familiar patterns and behaviors throughout your interface to build user confidence.
  • Feedback: Provide clear and immediate feedback for user actions to create a sense of control and understanding.
  • Accessibility: Design for all users, considering various abilities and contexts of use.

Law of Common Region

Elements tend to be perceived into groups if they are sharing an area with a clearly defined boundary.

Law of Similarity

The human eye tends to perceive similar elements in a design as a complete picture, shape, or group.

Law of Proximity

Objects that are near, or proximate to each other, tend to be grouped together.

Understanding these fundamental laws helps designers create interfaces that align with how people naturally perceive and process information. By applying these principles consistently, you can reduce the learning curve for new users and create more intuitive digital experiences.

Application in Design

  • Visual Hierarchy: Use size, color, and spacing to guide users' attention to the most important elements first.
  • Progressive Disclosure: Present information gradually, showing only what's necessary at each step to prevent overwhelming users.
  • Recognition Over Recall: Make objects, actions, and options visible rather than requiring users to remember information.
  • Flexibility and Efficiency: Provide shortcuts and customization options for experienced users while maintaining simplicity for beginners.

Law of Gestalt Laws of 6

People perceive and interpret ambiguous or complex images as the simplest form possible.

Law of 5

The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.

Law of 6

The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.

By incorporating these UX laws into your design process, you create products that not only look good but also feel natural and effortless to use. The goal is to reduce friction, increase user satisfaction, and ultimately create digital experiences that users love and return to.

Law of Common Laws of 4

Users spend most of their time on other sites, and they prefer your site to work the same way as all the other sites they already know.

Law of Similarity

Any task will inflate until all of the available time is spent, so set clear deadlines and constraints.
